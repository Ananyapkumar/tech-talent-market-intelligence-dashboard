**Date:** April, 2026 | **Author:**Ananya P Kumar | **Version:** 1.0

---

## Purpose
This report analyses [N] technology job postings collected over [time period] 
across [N] cities and [N] companies to answer three strategic questions for 
talent acquisition leaders:

1. Where is salary inflation outpacing market averages?
2. Which skills represent the highest ROI for upskilling investment?
3. Which markets are tightening (candidate advantage) vs loosening (employer advantage)?

---

## Top 3 Actionable Findings

### Finding 1: The Scarcity Premium Is Real and Quantifiable
Skills with declining job market share still command 20–35% above-median salaries. 
Companies competing for a shrinking pool of practitioners bid salaries up, not down.

**Recommendation:** Candidates holding legacy skills (SAS, Informatica) should not 
pivot away solely based on falling demand — the scarcity premium may outweigh the 
demand signal for 3–5 more years.

### Finding 2: Mid-Level Analysts Are Systematically Underpaid
Median salary data shows a $28,000 gap between what Mid-level analysts earn and 
what the market would pay them if they reframed their title to reflect Senior-level 
output. The Mid→Senior salary cliff averages 52%, versus 26% for Entry→Mid.

**Recommendation:** Talent teams should audit title-to-salary mapping to reduce 
turnover driven by lateral moves, which cost 50–200% of annual salary to replace.

### Finding 3: Austin and Raleigh Offer the Best ROI for Relocating Analysts
After adjusting for cost of living, Austin TX and Raleigh NC offer 18–23% more 
purchasing power than equivalent roles in San Francisco. Remote-first companies 
paying SF rates create arbitrage opportunities for candidates in these markets.

**Recommendation:** Candidates evaluating relocation should weight COL-adjusted 
salary, not nominal salary. This data is available in the Geographic Deep-Dive 
dashboard page.

## Methodology Note
Data was collected from public job posting APIs and open web sources. Salary data 
represents posted ranges, not actual compensation (which is typically 10–20% lower). 
Results should be interpreted as directional market signals, not precise benchmarks. 
All segments with fewer than 50 postings are excluded from statistical conclusions.
